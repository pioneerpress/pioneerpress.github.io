Little Anthony

     Hi Heather?  Tsup?  Ya got a minute?  Oh, is this your prep?  Sorry.
     Thanks, just a couple things:  first, if you have any extra glue sticks for our hallway board, that'd be awesome.  Oh cool.  I can come down there on my prep.
     Say, you've seen my Anthony, right?  Not Gina's Anthony.  That's Big Anthony.  Well, sorta medium, but anyway.  This's my Little Anthony.  In MY kindergarten.  Anthony with the blonde hair like The Little Prince about two feet tall?  Maybe not 'cause he's dropped off 45 minutes late every day by his mom.  Oh my God, his mom.  Shelley, Sheila?  I dunno.  She stinks like an ashtray with beer in it!  You don't see him at breakfast 'cause it's put away by time he gets here so I save him one, just some muffins, cereal, milk, and juice and give it to him when he gets here.  Right?  That one?  Well if you haven't seen him, I'm not surprised, he's so little.
     God, his mom!  She is such a I-don't-know, skank, you have to see her drop him off, 'cept she doesn't come in the building, now it's October, she figures he knows how to go to the office and get an admit slip himself, so she doesn't get talked to by secretary Terry.  But my god the clothes she wears, these little booty-shorts showin' off her little butt cheeks, cowboy boots and ripped-up T shirts like from some pull tab bar or Tiffany's the night before.  Really, I'm so serious, and she stinks like an ashtray mixed with old beer.
     Anyway, Little Anthony.  I gotta get him observed by the nurse and social worker 'cause he's just such a little wafer boy!  He's too young to be in schoo-ool!  I mean, he just turned five for god's sake and he's so dirty, same T shirt every day, his nose is running, he's always putting stuff in his mouth.  Just today?  Oh my god, I gotta call you back.
     Heather, that you again?  I was talking to you about his mom and him, and the whole entire time he was under his table with Playmobil stuff, and my class is at lunch, poor guy!  Crap, he's gonna tell his mom everything!  Heather, I gotta talk to the principal 'fore I get in trouble!  Oh my God.  Or I know.  I'll call his mom.  Dang, it's Sheila or Shelly.  I'll look it up.
     So yeah, he missed half his lunch already, and he needs to eat about three lunches, but he doesn't have any money in his account so they just give it to him because he won't bring back the frickin' free-and-reduced form for the third time.  But the ladies are gonna see his mouth is covered with marker!  His lips are all bluish yellow and his tongue is orange!  He keeps putting stuff in his mouth, Heather!  It's -- I'm out of my mind.  It's hard enough getting past morning meeting or freakin' guided writing without him putting everything in his mouth!  Oh, Heather.
     Between him, and this Mayweather, Tanisha's kid, who wipes feces on the wall, and Vang being checked out for pervasive developmental disorder, or whatnot, I dunno.  And that boy runs!  You can't keep him in your room without a leash.  And we gotta get these bulletin boards up for open house and grades!  Holy crap, dude, Heather!
     I know.  It's too much.  Please can you check out Little Anthony sometime?  You gotta see him.  His little blonde hair--well now she buzzed him so he looks like a lice treatment.  But just look out the front windows around 9:30 and see her car.  She parks in the bus zone in front.  Yeah, she drives like an El Camino or some damn thing.  Huh?  I'm kidding, oh it's like a Chevy car pickup truck thingy from the 70s.  Skip it, but oh my God.  
     Now I gotta go call his mom, then prob'ly talk to the principal, social worker and nurse.  He's so-o nee-eedy.  Well, I wasted your whole prep, so I should let you go!  But I can't because I gotta come down there and get those glue sticks.  See ya in a bit.  Thanks, Heather.

Flowing out at sunset

     Hissing a block away, tires and fenders surf over tar.  A million late-summer leaves flap, spin and strain against mature stems, like sequins on a Broadway marquee, tempting fate.  They give each other mighty applause.  Birds are down, last calls of day.  Sky is so high and powdery, evenly moist, but I can't touch it to verify.  It might be evenly dry.  They say the Delta Aquarids meteor shower played out against a black, moonless canopy last night.  I missed it.
     Why do I feel like a cropped tree that's lost so many limbs?  I'm drying, curling, and crumpling.  Loss of padding in joints, the hips ache, synapses... myelin sheathes are thinning.  Oh well. But I do.  I feel more alone, more likely to sit up in alarm at the announcement of another death, another passing.  I read strangers' obituaries.  Well, sometimes.

Not one second longer

     I'm a public special education teacher facing a crisis. a something I'm ashamed to tell you. i've lost the will.  
     the telling alone begs new punctuation for stuttered, fractured phrases. uncapitalized initial letters. periods followed by one space.  
     i know the target-market for this boo-hoo sob story is way limited, that no one reads it or gives a shit if people are living it.
     so. i don't think I can do the job one second longer. i've added/deleted, typed/erased my reasons why, but no reason covers it. i don't know what to do. get over myself? How in the hell did it get like this?
     'course it began with the workload, the incessant, drubbing threats of reprisal for not being caught up on paperwork. do special education teachers really get into trouble for not having caught up on their paperwork? i'm not in trouble. now. just internal. EasyIEP database has green checks, yellow triangles, red stop signs. they email you with condescending smileys and ask you what's your hold-up? do you need help? if you look at the numbers of compliance drones out there, checking, interweaving the staffs, crossing you off (do they play Russian-style hockey?) themselves also the subject of cross-checks by other administrators, then yes. you can get into trouble. you can be visited by suits, your meetings joined by iPads with suits. and PACER rep's. from the State of Minnesota. everyone praising, acknowledging, conceding that you really do so much! it's breathtaking how much you get done in the service of her Majesty, Queen Legalese.  why shucks, boss, why shucks.
     it continued the last three years with incessant vigilante cross checkers looking at your work to see if your language flowed from assessment to service minutes, from wide disparity between intellectual and academic achievement, from present levels to needs to goals to objectives, and then all bets off if the kid up and bombed or soared. three years ago, they used to check every single damn thing you wrote. you got called down to the office because inconsistencies arose (of-fracking-course they arose! they're human beings!) kids were doing too well, too poorly for their paperwork which is adjusted throughout the year anyway.
     i realize this is boring as hell.
     and then last year's mother of all clusters, kids dumped into grade levels 8-10 years beyond their abilities. i taught five-year-olds. i know why you don't place a typical five-year-old who's learning how to skip count (1-3-5, 2-4-6) into eighth grade math, or a brilliant five-year-old into fifth grade reading even if he could read The Goblet of Fire. administration didn't know/care if IEP minutes jibed with assessments of need. if an IEP said a kid read at second grade level but they forced him into 11th grade English, they're not going to know or care if the kid's IEP didn't provide enough support! no way. now suddenly administration recklessly forces you to break the law. two years ago, you were called on the carpet about inappropriate minutes of service. now you're forced to break the law in the name of... drumroll... equity. gasp. the new, never-heard-of-THAT mantra.
     to ensure equity, you're observed. you're observed 18 - 22 times. no one says why. oh. well. yes, you're being observed by administration in the building to see what students' active engagement looks like. what culturally relevant teaching looks like. pfft. but they don't ever tell you. no announcement. no "hey, we're coming on Thursday!" no feedback. no result. and you get why a reading coach observes you once a month for all nine months. she gives actual feedback. come to think of it, how'd she manage all the feedback she gave? and the sum result of her visits to see if you followed the curriculum was to dump the curriculum. mother-cluster. all those months spent learning the curriculum to dump it in favor of new curriculum you've been nominated to adopt, at summer training! cluster-mother. stupid Keystone Cops mentality. run around in a scrum. gather fake data.
     Then come 8-10 observations by the new special ed. supervisor who wouldn't call a student a student. s/he's a scholar now. mm-hm. and you're a genius. it's her cavalier trail blazing: "I'M THE FIRST PERSON ON EARTH TO DISCOVER INEQUITY IN EDUCATION and she will call a student a scholar! no, she never case managed a student nor taught a class, but she'll judge whether or not you can do it! she'll micro-manage your vernacular, stick her finger in your face to tell you what words you may and may not say [yes, really happened].
     Anyway, I'm in a shutdown, traumatized mindset. i feel fish hooks under my skin. i've stuck it out 15 years, eight of them in special education, and I don't know if I can do it. one. second. longer.

The Lord Ruler of Bubbles

Also, centipedes, spiders, and two-headed snapperish wigglers

     Lots of bubbles popping, some of them rising in clear, clean, bathtub realm, others drifting and sinking, in greasy, grimy, sloppy, tired dirigibles.  It's a poetry day, I think.  This happens when, when?  I don't know.  Fatigue rolls alongside my 12-year-old squeakmobile in his old Beverly Hillbillies jalopy.  It's two cars poking apace down the road, the me and the other.  Fatigue crawls along like Christmas Past, dragging chains and locks without keys.  Exhaustion generates opiates while I'm allegedly up, moving, all systems go, operational.  You can shake your head, but you just rearrange the dusty chandeliers.
     I like that:  dusty chandeliers.  
     I was thinking about all the centipedes and spiders we relocated yesterday in our basement.  You can't imagine.  There are cobwebs spanning windows and every angled corner, black ones heavy with weight of 1,000 cobweb passengers on top of them.  There is tomb-like dust on top of ductwork, dust so gritty and thick, you don't want to upset it, even with your dust rag and Endust can in your hand.  You're like, "Oh my God."  You touch it and it falls like sand in the Cask of Amontillado, into your eyelids and inside your collar.  Again.  You're accosted by the same trespasser guilt of one in a cemetery, like Balthasar scared away from Juliet's vault by Romeo.
     Anyway, when you scare off bugs, they simply scatter, empty the place awhile, then slowly creep back in.  I thought about their dark underworld, if it was too wet or dry, cold or warm, what's for lunch for them, and whether they had comfort, strains, longings, or pity.  I had pity for them, and thought of Robert Burns' sweet Ode to a Mouse.  "Wee, sleekit, cow'ring, timorous beastie!"  Well, except for the ones that were neither centipede nor spider, that seemed to have pinchers or heads on either end and wiggled away.  Those, I didn't like, but was too disgusted to squish.  The reaction this human had to that dank earth-world is as Balthasar by Juliet's vault.  Aghast, voyeuristic, afraid of terrible forebodings.  One wonders if Balthasar, Paris, Friar Lawrence, all saw a future resting place for their own bones, and bridled at the thought, pulling away from those shadows impelling Romeo and Juliet into the earth.

Morning, and a mobile construction

     As one who stands on a ladder and steadies in place the rustic birds and fish on a carved wooden mobile construction, or stops a leaf, a bird, a fish, a feather, a pipe cleaner, or a musical quarter note on a staff between his flat palms and rubs the palms slowly together, then watches the bird, leaf, fish, or feather pirouette from all sides, the pipe cleaner in its fuzzy angles, the quarter note on its rhythmic staff, its 3D placeholder, all gyrating on an airborne mobile, my awareness goes out toward life at dawn, then makes that distance commitment to that placeholder, that apprehension, sizing up object of attention, and senses loss of dream-freedom, loss of event horizon -- potentiality.  Absurdly, I envision wealth distribution at birth, wealth distribution at marriage, and greatest distribution of assets at death, but I can't make sense of why I see these things.  Things flowing toward everlasting centers, equalizing all.  All people moving towards a flagpole, a maypole celebration, which I never did.  I see a knothole on a tree, and the same knothole on my leather elbow.  And I wonder how my elbow became that off my watch, outside my purview.  All abstract thought pops into me at dawn, or perhaps while one is high on opiates.  Awakening through the limb consciousness is an opiate.  It's my cheap opiate. 
     Yet I'm still emptying the loss of extra consciousness out of me, sensing brightening, yet darkening of the mind. All goes out and in.  Awhile ago I saw disorganized, disheveled, happy shapes and colors.  Nothing sensible, just soft shapes undulating upward toward our dark closet.  Maybe as a baby sees his mobile above his crib.  The way Juju saw the doctor's black blood pressure cuff tubing coiled against a white wall.  He pointed out she was hypnotized.  The dark-light contrast excites the cerebral cortex and fires synapses and she laughs and loses a little saliva.  She forms baby concepts of matter, size, distance, velocity.  The greater the contrast of surroundings, the dark-light sensation, the more explosive her laughter.  It's all too fun!

Boyhood & Girlhood

     We saw "Boyhood" last night at The Lagoon. What an epic melding of real actors' lives with a totally believable, fictional thread of family life. The director himself apparently had no idea if the actors would stick it out as long as they did (12 years?).  I guess his daughter didn't want to, but she hung in there. After 2 hours, 40 minutes, we stood on Lagoon Ave outside, amidst the parade of uptown revelers, guys on scooters, girls hiking skirts with beers in hand and cigarettes in teeth, and jumping onto dumb guys' backs, drunken shouts, and we just ran down scene after scene after scene. "Could you believe it when?"  Loved it as much as last year's "Blue is the Warmest Color." Things it lacked: car crashes, balls of fire, Transformers, and nobody blowed up! I told our friend, the credits were short; they had no CGI people, no FX people! It was all just lighting, sound, and music.  I always feel like Ethan Hawke plays himself, but others thought he was great, especially when he played a piano lullaby early on, and later when he said, "I've finally turned into the castrated guy your mom always wanted me to be."  Patricia Arquette was stunning, and kept reminding me of the mother of a friend of our daughter.  The boy played by Ellar Coltrane embodies all this indecision, sensitvity, and malleability.  He's really a vessel we might pour ourselves into, and walk along in our lives.  But I thought his sister, played by Lorelei Linklater, if she'd had as much screen time as him, could've changed the title to "Boyhood and Girlhood."  She was absolutely as compelling, and probably had untapped dimensions to her character.  Maybe that was a facet that unfortunately fell out on the cutting room floor over 12 years, that she did not want to be part of it as much as he did.  The changes they go through physically and emotionally remind you that we're all almost completely different people at 18 than we were at age 7.

Anyway, yeah, see it.

School's Greatest Hits

     Okay, I'll preface "School's Greatest Hits."  They're a series of true school fights I remember from Minneapolis and Saint Paul schools.  I've changed the names.  Why would I focus just on fights, without explaining antecedents, circumstances, without interventions, support, or consequences for these students' actions?  I'm hoping that by throwing them up here on this blog, I talk straight to the reader's sensitivity about how much fighting goes on, and just get people to ask why.  Can something be done about it?  Bottom line is, I've examined my limited experiences with fights in my life, with my biases, and I haven't been able to stop school's greatest hits from keeping on coming.  Finally, I don't exactly know why I tell them in isolated fashion, but that's what I'm doing.  So check it out.

     Michael and Christopher took their backpacks off and squared up in the front corner of third grade class.  They were so tired of each other's taunts.  Michael constantly running it on Christopher, "He-wear-a-diaper gon'-cry-about-it," Christopher "black-as-black-gets," and Christopher saying, "You better watch your mouth," and the teacher intervened, and Michael said, "How he gon' come at me sayin' stuff?" and "I ain't havin' it," and then WHOMP! Michael ripped a left hook that spun Christopher's jaw like a bobblehead, Christopher looked around without wincing a bit, charged headfirst into Michael's bigger chest, knocking the American flag off the wall.
     Third grader Phillip was so fed up with being teased all school year, "Faggot!  You Gay!  Ya faggot mama lovin gay boyyyy!  He ain't go do nothin', pillow poochy fat one!"  Till he finally did with a low growl, then a high-pitched squall, and went swinging in every direction, smacking Leander right in the face, and Dante in the shoulder, a couple girls in their breasts, and spiral notebooks off desktops.  Everyone stood back.  It looked like a mini Tasmanian Devil tornado.
     Fourth graders Cyn'thea and Jasmine flew at each other on the bus before school after Cyn'thea grabbed Jasmine's big braids on top of her head while she proceeded to the back of the bus, and straight-up pulled her flat down on the floor.  Jasmine squirmed up and threw every finger and boot heel at the equally swinging Cyn-thea.  No one knew why it started.
     Third grade white boy Anthony at another school got drop-kicked by Johnson coming off the slide at recess, was knocked back rolling, sick for air, then went on, playing soccer, and caught a ball in the face off Johnson's foot at close range.  Anthony didn't do anything about it.  He did cry that time.  He didn't try anything to get back at Johnson, though.  He quietly told the teacher who called Anthony's mom, who said she understood, and thanks for calling.  Johnson told his friends, "Anthony soft.  He soft."
     Handsome Beandre in fourth grade taunted most of the boys who interfered or argued with his brags, and he acted like the whole wide world was his toy store.  Until Exeter, new boy transfer, who repeatedly muttered, very softly, "Who he?  He ain't nothin'."  Staff came running to the hall by the bathroom where Exeter had "stole off" (cold-cocked) Beandre's nose, gushing red everywhere, but now Beandre had Exeter down on the floor, blue uniform shirt over Exeter's head, and was repeatedly stomping his face.
     Large, smiling, 10th grade L'Vaughnta, and his EBD gangbanging cousin Stinson, needed cash money.  They stalked and cruised the small-stature Karen crew around the ELL room at the high school.  One of the Black boys, they forgot who, maybe Leshawn, had said the Karen crew had come up on him in the bathroom while he was peeing, and surrounded him, although no punches were thrown.  At least L'Vaughnta thought he heard that.  And his boy Leshawn wouldn't lie.  L'Vaughnta had already been suspended for one fight, and telling a teacher who wanted his pass in the hallway to "Get off my dick 'fore I fuck you up."  They chose one little boy sitting on the marble steps and beat him so badly his eye nearly popped out.  They forgot to demand any money.
     Anesha wasn't having it anymore with Tatiana.  They used to be friends.  They used to take the bus to the Mall of America, just to steal little stuff.  Now she saw Tatiana dancing with D'Ray, her ex, at the middle school dance.  Anesha walked right up behind Tatiana on the dance floor, opened her fist, wound her hand all the way back to Chicago, and exploded it on Tatiana's cheek.  You could hear that firecracker sock over the dubstep beat.  It's too bad, because that was a good song to dance to.
     La'Crezius beats all.  This fifth grader on the north side of Minneapolis was La Craziest.  When he got mad, his mouth foamed, spit flew, he growled accusations, and would pace the room.  You couldn't talk him down.  He threw chairs, one out the second storey window.  He knocked one kid out with three chair smacks to the top of the head.  The police came and took him for awhile.  His seat was close to the door in case it was time to get him out of the room.  He was in a horrible foster home two blocks from school where his "mom" beat him with her high heels.  His forehead was dotted with round high heel scars.  When the police took him, probably home, he somehow got back to the school grounds, and he stood outside, down on the grass.
     The teachers all heard him.  La'Crezius looked like a bawling Spartacus down there, legs in superhero pose, both hands repeatedly shoving middle fingers up in the air at his classroom windows.  "Fuck you!  And fuck you!"  His teacher calmly told him to go home.  He had pleaded with the police not to take him home.  "And fuck you, too!"  Tears streamed down.  He kept jabbing his fingers up in the air.  
     These are some of schools' greatest hits.

Sled dogs

     Sometimes I see the scuffle and scuttle of preparation for writing on this screen as a flurry and bother, and I see an Inuit running to catch his own dog team pulling his sled away in completely excited abandonment.  Like, "Hey wait!  Hold up!"  Especially if other things keep interrupting me, and of course, those are things of my own creation!
     I have an idea for another vignette, in itself a montage of vignettes of kids punching one another, of all the punches I've been unable to stop.  Now, I know!  I know!  That's a horrible thought!  Why write about that?  I just had this Catcher in the Rye sense, that I was failing at catching all the kids as they ran through the rye and over the cliff while singing, "If a body [catch] meet a body, coming through the rye."  What good would that serve, that montage of punches?
     I don't know, maybe the simple writing of it would exorcise my heart of all the traumas I've endured without respite, without an ear to express my fears and anger.  I have so much anger at my school, and maybe it is just MY school that failed miserably at immersing all Federal Setting 3 students into the mainstream.  No, I know all the middle schools, except maybe for my daughter's Monroe, were absolute hellholes.  
     Several kids on my own caseload were completely removed from Saint Paul schools for inflicting beatings so severe that one defenseless victim nearly lost his eye.  A friend of one Karen victim asked his ELL teacher why that perpetrator was allowed to come back to school (until he did it again), and the ELL teacher shook his head and told the poor kid he didn't know why.
     Anyway, I'm scared.  I'm scared of all the trauma reoccurring without preparation or solution or upward feedback to change policies.  I don't know if I can take it anymore.  And I can't care less, or just pop a "I-Don't-Give-a-Shit" pill every morning to ease my stress levels.  They've allowed two EBD teachers in our building to get out of EBD and into LD case management, which tells me two things:  1) THEY don't want to manage behaviors so intense anymore, and 2) supervisors downtown will want LD teachers to stop bitching about case managing OHD and LD students with unidentified mental illnesses.  They can just say, "Who are you to complain?  So 'n so case manages worse behaviors than you have, and they don't complain."  Which is just a clusterfucking setup.
     But I've been my only catcher in the rye since walking into Hall Community school in north Minneapolis in 1999, and the principal, now Mpls superintendent, greeted me with, "I got you?  I don't get a say?  Well, Mr.  OL-SON, go to it, get up there to your classroom, and go to it."  She was an imperial dictator.  I've never met a nastier, more unstable, megalomaniacal bully of a principal.  And now she's in charge of Mpls schools.
     I had 95% African American students, and team taught third grade in the same room all day with an African American woman who really was marginally qualified as an instructor out of the St. Mary's Collaborative Urban Educator (CUE) program.  I remember her terrible grammar and spelling, but her street cred with kids was immense.  I remember her telling a kid in a whispered growl to put something down, saying, "Pet it dan!"  She got immediate compliance.  Police came to our room several times that year due to one mentally ill boy, Lumasakila, whom I had to restrain on the floor, who threw chairs, who picked up a chair and repeatedly beat another boy on the head with it.  I flew over three desks to tear it out of his hands.  Lumasakila had little round scars on his forehead from being beaten by his foster mother's high heels.  At one point I was ordered to walk him daily off the grounds at dismissal.  
     I could go on and f*ing on.  And on.  This has upset my stomach and legs, which I haven't moved from their crossed position, and they're starting to whine at me now to get up and get off this subject.  But the problem was not Lumasakila.  It was not a single one of those lovely nine-year-olds.  They were so great.  I remember trying to stop the boys from doing their incredible gymnastic flips on the playground for fear someone was going to break his neck.  I remember the great double-dutch jumproping recesses.  They were fantastic kids.  Wish I could remember more.  But we do tend to remember the kids with high-heel scars tattooed across their foreheads.  Because the school would not report that foster mom.  She scared the shit out of our tough-ass Black principal!  The now superintendent who bullied staff with crazymaking flipflopping policies.
     I have to stop now.  But I am going to draft something of a terrible, greatest hits of violence to expose it.  It's not the kids -- well, I'm not naive enough to excuse all kids from being monsters -- there are monsters out there, future monsters who might not make it to age 18, nor stay out of jail.  I've been working with the ones trying trying trying to keep them out of jail.  But it is primarily the district's own damn fault with its chronic ignorance of classroom reality, denial of teacher concerns, its dictatorial bullying of principals and PLC TOSAS, and recalcitrance to doing anything about behavior.  Then to pay hundreds of thousands, millions to Pacific Education Group so that it spews platitudes about over-representation of Black boys in special education (like we don't know it -- f*ing duh!) about what would be great to recognize where teachers sit with their own biases, their own so-called "truths" and "discomfort" and "nonclosure," where they are "on the compass," all such lovely platitudes if you're not in front of a classroom where beat-downs are scheduled for YouTube recording in the hall after class, and the onsite administration doesn't know what to do about because they're being bullied by Special Education supervisors, or because of their own exhaustion with dozens of incomplete initiatives having something to do with data.
     Oh God, you wonder why I'm angry and tired.  It's not the kids that wear me out.

     Retiring professor Sue Howard's final words to teacher candidates:  "So my lesson to you, dear young teachers, going out there, is to talk to someone when you know you're not just normally rundown, and it's not just normal sleep deprivation.  Go find your enchanted well of love and support, tap into it, hold it close to your heart.  Take the summers off.  Spend time with adult peeps.  Hit the road in a convertible and get out of town!"

     Fred had never in his life felt so recognized, like an aura rippled around him, like the youngest player in the Van Cliburn competition, bolstered and primed to dive into the spirits of five-year-olds.  

     He had thrown his guts into that calling, but five years later, the year of Sue's retirement, his soul fed through a junkyard car crusher, he could barely finish the year.

     Sue saw Fred before her retirement at an annual spring block party.  She was caring for a young autistic boy on the block whose parents took respite away from him one week every month.  
     Fred stood barefoot in the middle of the still-warm black street arranged with grills, coolers, and picnic tables, with his wife and daughter, under a setting yellow sun.  Water balloons had popped, had left their red and blue rubber strands around, and water glistened the tar.  His flat feet made farting sounds as he moved from Lays potato chips to brownies, blondies, and lemon bars.

     "Yes, ha-hi!"  His voice quavered a little.

     "Yeah, it's okay."

Ghost in the school

     The teacher lies awake on his left side before dawn, his spastic tip of tongue touching roof of mouth, fingers cupped in swimmer's hands, back-of-hands together, eyes peeled, elbows in and knees balanced wobbly on top of each other, on left side like a cracked-open clam.  The Adam's apple drops and rises.  He shakes himself out of limp blankets, and goes to work.  
     He leans forward, says "Hi" to colleagues also stooped and heading inside, into the badly grafted architecture, a floor plan with sprawling limestone wings added to early 20th century red brick.  Did they say "hi" back?  Huh.  He didn't hear anything.
     Head buried in tabs, four kids' cases projected into a 13 inch white tablet of fields and bubbles, hearing the slave-ship oarmaster's mallet pounding a wood post, that angry face bellowing "Stroke!" -- this mindsucking screentime for 75% of the day, he rises from his stained-upholstery, plum-colored office chair whose wheels clatter on the broken-tile floor, only to chat with kids about homework, why they didn't do their classwork, his saintly wait time for answers, anticipating their state tests, lying to them that tests matter, simpering, allowing, compromising himself, making headfakes and half grimace-smiles to them.  Do they know he's got their backs?  He can't tell.  He can't suss their attitudes.  They don't see him, but he sees them.  Is he even there?  He loops gently from desk to desk with torn, skidding soles, offering soft encouragement or admonishments.  Did they hear him?  They're talking to each other.  He knits his eyebrows, looks up, shuts eyes, sighs, turns, and scuffs back to his beaten-down desk and iPad.  
     Head jacks back into legalese screentime.
     He can't use the bathroom before meeting with the hard driving, real content teachers.  They're the real teachers.  His role is to bend and warp instruction for disabled children.  Lacking their eight hours a week to dedicate to one class and PLC, he lacks.  He just lacks.  They laugh and giggle scattershot, salting the tiny cubicle with sarcasm about what should be taught or could be learned, but punt and dump four core standards just added last week, and how a data cycle, that thing they hate and hiss at, could still be completed by the following Friday.  A grinning equity poster blats out platitudes.  They just keep talking and snarking.  He doesn't know what's funny.
     He can't keep up.  Treads water in ankle chains, shallow breathing, volleys of mandates scratching exclamations down his backside.
     When he rises and walks out of the conference room, he says, "Thanks, catch you later," but no one answers.  They keep talking and laughing.  Did they hear him say catch you later?  Why can't anyone hear or see him?  He looks at his hands to check his complexion; to see if he's fading, turning transparent.  He can't see himself.  What's happened?  Kids flow past like hot breeze, like window raindrops around him.  Will he be knocked flying?
     He knows.  He's been fading for weeks.  Celluloid life strips fly out of his head projector and melt before touching the floor.  The bank is on negative empty.  Gas tank on fumes.  He talks to former teachers, holding great chats about the past, shaking his head about kids who ended up senators or dead, about long-dead teachers who used to smoke in this lounge, and silly eduspeak like "cascades of fidelity," "sandboxing ideas," or "speaking one's truth" behind a gray iron curtain.
     He eats a tuna sandwich and someone says hi, he smiles, mouth closed, looks up, they're helloing someone else.
     He finds a second to pee, and blinks that skin lives under his clothes.  Thank God or Whoever, he's a person.  At the end of the day, backpack straightening his shoulders, he can't find his key to lock the classroom.  The office is empty and shadowed.  His name is gone from the mailbox.  His eyes hover frantically over 58 other mailboxes.
     His tongue back on the roof of his mouth, hands cupped like swimmers', and panting shallowly, he tilts forward to his car, ordering his feet to feel the ground.  Please don't disappear.

Suzi Walks-On-Cement

     Suzi Walks-On-Cement always liked to put her hands on two desks, lift off the floor, and swing her body.  I told her not to do it.  Then I told her be careful.  Then I didn't.
     Suzi was a third-grade city transplant from northern Minnesota, moved in with her dad in Saint Paul.  She was Ojibwe from Red Lake Indian Reservation, where her mom still lived with a boyfriend, and her older brother.
     "Can I use the phone to call my mom 'cause she gonna pick me up and take me back to the rez?" Suzi Walks-On-Cement asked me.  Suzi averted her eyes to speak, rarely made eye contact, but when done talking, looked up with her church window forehead, and squinted from the sun behind me.  She really, really wanted permission.  Her eyes shifted till she found mine, and she bore in.  At all other times... I mean.  All.  Other.  Times.  I didn't exist.  I really didn't.  She wouldn't read aloud, she wouldn't do homework, write a letter, do math.  Anything.  I noticed today's spaghetti sauce spilled on her dirty white T shirt.
     "Suzi, you keep asking me that every day, but over lunch or from the office.  I don't want kids --"
     "Then I'll just go and use the phone in the office."  And Suzi marched with mad soldier hands out the portable room door, down the makeshift wooden steps, out the cement sidewalk to the office in the building.   Suzi walked on cement.
     She always said her mom was coming to take her back from her dad, but her dad had just taken her from her mom. 
     "I just wanna get back up with my mom," she said.  "I ain't staying here."  Mom did stuff with her.  
     "She gonna let me do whatever I want.  We goin' to the ca-see-no, yah.  She gonna buy me some nicer clothes."
     Suzi really, really wanted to be with her mom; she needed her mom.  I told her that.  Still, she sat on the floor and didn't use her chair, and wouldn't do what the class did.
     Christopher, this other Native kid, shrugged his shoulders when she walked out the portable room door.  He said, "I'm Hochunk Indian and you don't see me up an' and out of here just cuz I'm Indian.  School don't bother Hochunk."
     Someone: "Suzi just wants to be with her mom."  
     Someone: "Yeah, leave her alone, dude."  
     Someone: "Her mom's coming.  She told me."
     I never saw Suzi again.  
     Back in November, like Suzi, I'd been been bumped into that third grade portable, bumped hard, from another building.  They just took away old keys, and handed me new keys.

     "Nah, man," Suzi said.  "I don't give a shit about that."

When thanks come from me

     Rarely do I have it, but when I do, I have to give it back, give thanks; I have to tend this spirit, this garden, or else it'll waste into selfishness; so, thank you sun for flowing so buttery into our room today.  The light spectrum might have moved off blue, moved off white, moved onto cream, and might be due to forest fires out west or particulates, even pollution.  How did Joni Mitchell describe that light? 
And the neon mercury vapor stained
Miami sky
It's red as meat
It's a cheap pink rose, 
I have to elicit praise and thanks for the pretty glow.  I don't... I don't often give thanks to Whoever, tiny spirit or fragile faith that I have, but I want to say thanks to earth or H.P. (higher power) for a little thing like this.
     There are other reasons, if I pause to grow a little prayer.  The great luck to wake up -- again! -- and crack my ankles.  They cracked about 13 times!  The opportunity to feel the soft chenille blanket glide over me, the temperature just right for my narrow mammal needs.  To loll and nod for awhile longer.  The happy accident to just be in this fairly healthy body, at my age! where all my parts still work.  The good fortune not to be in chronic pain.  The sweet luck of possessing any gratitude, seeing how crabbish I can roam.  And I'll "a stately pleasure-dome decree," to borrow Coleridge from Kubla Khan, wherein, it felt like he could've been talking about his mind.  
     Anyway, to drink another day of good luck, wholesomeness.  Thanks.

Curt Jamison, security guard

Characters:  
Curt Jamison (CJ) school security guard, police investigator (PI), school principal (S)


     CJ:  Hi, I'm Curt Jamison.
     PI:    Hi Curt.  Yes, have a seat anywh -- that's as good as any.  Good.  Now just relax.  Thank you for staying after to talk with us.  We have a few questions; that's all; you're not in any trouble.  Just I guess, start by telling us your job title here.
     CJ:  I'm one of four security guards at Crosstown High School.  My job is to check kids' passes, get them to class, basically round them up, keep the halls clear.  Or wherever the office tells me they want them to go when they're not where they're supposed to be, which lately I don't -- it isn't the easiest... do you have to write this down?
     Principal pokes head inside conference room door:  Curt, I'll want to talk to you when you're done with the police.
     CJ:  Nah-kay, that's fine.
     PI:    Well Curt, we just need a preliminary statement from all of the staff in connections with, all staff who helped the boy who was hurt here today.
     CJ:  Oh no, that's fine.  All I can tell you is what I saw.  I mean I came into the cafeteria looking for -- 
     PI:  Well let's first back up.  Have you ever had any contact with this boy before he hung himself during lunch today in the cafeteria ?
     CJ:  Um no, actually.  I don't think I'd ever heard of him.  He just transferred what -- like two weeks ago, I heard.
     PI:  Okay, well you may not be alone in not knowing much about him.  Had you heard that he was transferred to Crosstown due to uh, attempts at his last school, well, school attempts to protect him?
     CJ:  That's like no, that's the first I heard that.  They don't usually tell me -- see, 'cause my job is just to help kids get to class....
     PI:  Well since you put your hands on him, lifted him off the floor, albeit for what, well, I mean, you showed quick thinking and good instincts, and according to staff and a couple kids, now you just have to answer a few more questions.
     CJ:  I'd like to ask anybody in his right mind what he would've done watching a kid hang by a rope from the TV.  Gotta get the damn rope off his neck so I had to lift him up!  I mean, God.  How often you have to do that?  Everybody was just standing there.  Most kids in the cafeteria, even adults, didn't know anything was going on, I mean, you serious?
     PI:  Easy, okay?  You're fine; you're good.  You want a drink of water?  Here.
     CJ:  No, I just wanna know if I'm in trouble for having to lift a kid off the floor to get the rope off his neck.  I mean is he dead?
     PI:  Well, I can't tell you his current status, but to answer your first question, you're right; I don't think anyone would've thought to do anything differently.  Did you know you broke the other student's cell phone that was taking a video?
     CJ:  He was taking a video of an attempted suicide!  I just took it threw it on the floor.  How would this boy's mom feel when his attempted suicide got put up on YouTube?  I mean man you realize -- ?
     PI:  Okay, easy.  Easy.  You were just trying to help, but now you've got another family upset because you don't have permission to willfully damage student property.  We're also looking into the records on the hanging boy -- they show he tried it twice at his last school, and nobody thinks you could've known he had paperwork on how to handle him physically, his physical body.  But now we've got two families upset.  For sure, one is more upset because it's their son, but he was not to be manhandled due to his seizures, maybe guaranteed by loss of oxygen, and he went into a seizure after you touched him, which, I KNOW!  You don't make a regular part of your job duties to put hands on any kids.
     CJ:  I know that.  Jesus.  This situation was fairly chaotic, man.  
     PI:  What do you mean, chaotic?
     CJ:  I just mean I have to stay hands-off 99% of the time, and I never use force to break up fights.  Hell, look at me!  I'm a little dude!  And you cops got all the contact rights that I'm not supposed, that I don't.  So what are they saying?  Is the kid dead?
     PI:  Curt, we're not free to discuss the boy's condition.
     CJ:  Oh god, man.  You gotta be kidding me.  I killed a kid trying to save him?  I had him up off the floor so the rope could come loose, but nobody was helping me, man.  I was like screaming for help.  They just fucking stood there, man.
     PI:  You know, I think we've got all we need for this portion.
     CJ:  You don't have enough for ANYthing, man.  What portion?
     PI:  Officers here will come in now and take you downtown for a better interview.
     CJ:  Wha-why?
     PI:  Because everyone who had contact with him, the nurse, the assistant principal, don't worry, everyone has to come downtown.
     CJ:  I have a little girl I have to pick up from her school!  Let me at least call my wife.
     PI, to Principal:  He'll need to make a phone call and come with us, that okay?
     S:  Nah, that's good.

Palpitations

     11:16 am Palpitations, trying to find my way.  Hollow insides need evidence, clues on how to move, second to moment, not knowing what's  best.  
     I've written some poems while preoccupied with other tasks; a few vignettes when pressed to get it done; but I don't have a main charge.  I don't see my outlet, so I just keep running in circles, referencing past inspiration, searching for meaning in ancient works.  Then I spend all this hollow time searching, like a wizened beachcomber with his metal detector, covering acres of scorching white sand for nothing, maybe broken fishing lures and an occasional nickel, dime or quarter.  Inbetween these sentences, I just anxiously wiggle my ankle, unable to get back inside my body and stay there.
     1:03 pm. 
     And now it's 7:09.  Have several threads.  Just. Just.  Not anything that feels communicable. I know what I was going to say in the other draft.  I just often want to say it straight to an audience, thus I stick in lyrical voice, from me to you, demi-God?  Are you there -- portable spirituality?  Sometimes I call You my everlasting Whoever, minting my own religion.  Something I can pick up and swing over my shoulder to bring with me.  But I'm freaked there's no one there.  Utlimately, I hope my growing little body of work might begin to appreciate a kind of value.  Okay I'll get back to my other draft of fiction.  A teacher fiction.
     I didn't jump over to the other piece.  And now it's 7:45pm.  Maybe now....
