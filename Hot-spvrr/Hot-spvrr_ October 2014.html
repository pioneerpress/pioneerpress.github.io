Wednesday, October 22, 2014

panic

     You know what?  I've written the below and come back up here to preface:
     I keep thinking I'm going to expose feelings that were never before naked, like a rock tumbled out to the sun for the first time in its billion-year life by a farm tractor and harrow.  It's worked its way up from hell since 3.9 billion years ago, and ah, now, what is this warm, laser disc, the sun, but a flashback of one rock's own birth millennia ago.
     And the start.
     Ah, the comfort zone of lower case titles.  "panic."  Self-serving in its pursuit of calm.  
     This day ranked at the top with the highest anxiety days I've ever worked, from being observed by hostile district POWERS, who neither give a heads when or why they're coming, to a follow-up why, what they saw.  Maybe they're not out to find faults, or in the slightest manner of spying on staff at work.  Maybe.  Thing is, wtf do they want?
     Then that ended with smiles all round, and the clacking of high heels out of the room.
     In anticipation of a double family IEP/evaluation meeting this afternoon during my double prep, I attempted to print out my finished documents.  Ack, no internet this morning, after waiting a week for an evaluation to be completed by one of the contributors, so it appeared I couldn't print it, nor read it to a visiting mom about to arrive in hours.  Meantime -- love that term, "meantime," the double entendre: unkind time.  Well, dammit, it's true!  These are panic-attack provoking, explosive, dagger-tipped days, blaming days, where we see teachers being evaluated by people who don't really come to help, and don't have the time to offer any....
....
     I took a couple minutes break, came back, and this whole entry tastes so bitter in my mouth.  I mean, I can taste it.  Like the memory of pennies in my mouth as a kid.  Or the copper or brass window pulls over my baby crib.  I remember they tasted so cold and coppery.  The flavor of whacking your head on the cupboard, feeling that jolt saw down through the crevices in your cranium, or of accidentally touching a cord and tasting that metal shock, zigzagging through your mouth and teeth fillings.

entrances and exits

Basically my whole teaching career, the abridged version.
     I volunteered tutoring reading to 3rd graders at Saint Paul's Expo school in 1996, felt extremely gratified to be there, explored teaching programs at Saint Thomas, the U of M, elsewhere, attended UST, got licensed, a masters' degree, sub taught in Saint Paul.
     Applying in several districts, I got a third grade position at Hall school in North Minneapolis in 1999, which was led then by now-superintendent _____ _____.  When I introduced myself, she told me, "I get you?" meaning, no one told her HR would assign teachers for her vacancies.  She said, "Well, the room is upstairs, down the hall.  Have at it."  One day before classes.  The room had desks and dictionaries in it.  When I announced my leaving in early August the following year, ______ called me on my new cell phone and told me I was under contract not to quit with less than one month's notice, and said she would make it so that I would never work again, certainly never in MPS.
     Hired by Linwood A+ in Saint Paul 2000-1 school year, I took .5/.5 positions, teaching morning poetry as a specialist, and afternoon kindergarten, to fill spots emptied by their having cut their librarian.  The staff decided together the following spring to cut my half of kindergarten, and one said as the meeting wrapped up, "Well, that wasn't so bad, was it?" and I left the school, finding a full time kindergarten at Groveland Park.
     Groveland Park, 2001-2, also featured half-day kindergarten, and I taught one year there, enjoying increasing my skills.  When my afternoon kindergarten shrank to 12 kids by Halloween of 2002, the principal told me I needed to accept a half-time position at Highland Park, but after talking by phone to that principal, ____ _____, I decided I would never do another .5/.5, especially not at different sites.  My then-current principal ____ ____ pointed her finger into my chest, incredulous that I would not do what she said.
     When she followed by telling me I could have a full-time third grade in a portable at Highland, I took it and left on November 1, 2002.  I managed a wild classroom outside the main building, managing to segue to my preferred kindergarten the following fall.  Taking on kindergarten, I found the room stripped of furniture, manipulatives, and most books by a neighbor.  She and her kindie BFF -- older lady drinking buddies both  named _____ who had side-by-side cabins, put out newsletters without me, taught a curriculum comprised of little paper photo-copied books, and I was relegated to making my own curriculum that aligned with IB, or begging for additional copies from them.  _____ told me, when I said I needed to figure out curriculum, that I should create it, and not need "cookie-cutter lesson plans."  I injured my neck in the winter, had surgery on my C6-7, went on short-term disability, came back, and found my room taken from me despite having been given a clean bill of health.  I was given an office across the hall from which I would provide some resource role, and sub for other teachers as needed.  When I protested this assignment, ____ told I needed to leave the school and not come back, because if I attempted to return in the fall, I would be placed on an improvement plan from which I would not recover.  I sought support from the union, which told me to do what she said, and I filed a grievance with the state department of human rights for violating terms of short-term disability.  I also detailed a laundry list of sexual inappropriateness by _____, her telling numerous jokes throughout the year about breasts, penis size, men's buttocks, repeatedly mocking male staff, and her successful campaign to remove 4/5 male staff from a building of 26 female teachers.  The grievance was denied; the rationale that I hadn't lost any wages; however, "highly unethical employment practices had taken place."  No further information was provided by the state of Minnesota.  
     I moved to Museum Magnet in the Rondo building in 2003-4, and taught fourth grade for two years. The principal, _____ _____ was quite fair with me, and once reacted humorously to a story I told her about _____, saying, "She's got a lot of tits."  _____ retired.  I once looked back at my bosses in SPPS.  The Linwood principal, ____ ___, and Groveland principal, _____, both had left for administrative positions in American schools in the United Arab Emirates, _____ retired, _____ retired.  A few principals later, Linwood's _____ was later replaced by a UAE alum, _____ _____, who had never taught class nor served as assistant principal in a public school, and who has now bounced up to superintendent of Richfield schools.  
     I decided I could no longer thrive for 6 1/2 hours a day with the same kids, enrolled in a special education licensing program at Saint Thomas, got my second masters', finally taking a job in 2006 for the next eight years at Como Park High School, case managing students with learning disabilities.
     Now as I look at the stability I've achieved, my training and what I've accomplished as a teacher, I consider my next steps.  Which doorway will I exit through or enter?  I'm extremely exhausted and looking for another path.  It's never that the kids wipe me out. The problem rests totally in coping with time frames and conflicting, impossible demands.  Demands to observe, interview, test, and write due process paperwork without enough time to do it; to prep courses, deliver instruction, grade; to meet with a PLC team four periods per week; to meet with families about their children's evaluations & IEPs; to meet with staff, do conferences, and professional development.
     Possibly, the percentage requirement of due process amounts to 50% of my job; I steal from kids' needs to accomplish it.  I'd bet that tallying up the hours applied to due process would vary from week to week, but would certainly minimally top 10 hours, and maximally reach about 30 per week, if one includes the time spent seeking out a student, calling a parent and interviewing her; observing the kid, testing, interviewing him and his teachers, and writing summary reports and IEPs.  PLC amounts to 13 hours per month if you include 4 ⅓ weeks in a month, but SpEd teachers must get permission, order a sub, and write sub plans just to secure our 3.5 hours per month of due process time.  It's so hard to fight for respect for these duties, and that's just stupid silly.

Monday, October 6, 2014

Arm strength

     Don Kaufhold from across the highway liked to drive tractor the mile over to our yellow brick farmhouse and take the piss out on us every couple weeks, pushing my soft-spoken dad around till he was ready to say, "Get the hell off our property," Don resting his 275 lb, 6'4"-frame on our creaking oak dining room chairs, legs akimbo in his stinky orange milk parlor overalls.  Don rented acreage from my dad.  Every harvest he disputed the lay of the land, finally paying in installments, and every spring he decided to re-up and rent again.
     This grinding process of bitching over 37 or 38 acres gave my dad fits.
     Kaufhold had been jawing about the sheer arm strength he needed for certain farm feats.
     "Here, Teddy, I'll show you something," he said to me, pumping crazy black German eyebrows at my dad for permission, my dad answering with a skeptical smile.  "Come on outside.  I'll show you something."  Don was lit with circus strong-man verve.
     He had a metal pole, and asked me to grip it with one hand.  Now, when the end of a 6' round dowel, a solid steel cistern tank girder, rests heavily on your soft hand, and your knuckle cartilage stretches, and the rod doesn't quiver; instead, it wants to sink, fall to earth and stay there, and you know you're not going to lift it from anywhere but its fulcrum... the power lay beyond me.
     I grabbed hold toward the middle, and Don said, "Whoa, no ya don't.  Grab it down at the end."  I lifted my end, and tried to raise the other end, but couldn't do it, not even with both hands, the far end immediately falling with a clank to the ground, my 16-year-old neck bones popping in shock.  I laughed sheepishly:  "No.  Way."
     "Watch me now," Don said.  I watched his ruddy, gorilla fist grab that rod by the end and slowly budge its far end off the dirt driveway.  It came up.  His forearm tendons waved like a purple accordion.
     "Huh?  How's that, huh?" Kaufhold said, grinning through white teeth.  
     "What a dick," I thought.
     "You're pretty strong" was all I could muster before turning sideways at my dog, whose tail had stopped wagging, mouth had shut so it could focus, perked up for the next entertainment.  
     "Well, I got homework to do," I said.

     Set-up for the timid apprehension of a thing before me:  to stop, breathe, and apprehend reality.  But I can't loose the rod from its lowly place.  Can't even wield one end of the beast some nights.

Raferty the poet

Padraic Colum (1881–1972).  Anthology of Irish Verse.  1922.
 
142. I Am Raferty
 
By Douglas Hyde (Translated)
 
 
I AM Raferty the Poet
  Full of hope and love,
With eyes that have no light,
  With gentleness that has no misery.
 
Going west upon my pilgrimage        5
  By the light of my heart,
Feeble and tired
  To the end of my road.
 
Behold me now,
  And my face to the wall,        10
A-playing music
  Unto empty pockets.
 
Raferty, a Connacht peasant poet, while at some festivity, heard someone asking who he was. He was then blind and a fiddler. Turning around he made this perfect utterance. Raftery died in 1835. His poems have been collected, edited and translated by Dr. Douglas Hyde.
