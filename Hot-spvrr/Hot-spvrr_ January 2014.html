Monday, January 27, 2014

crunchy blue crystal frost

     Merciful lord, the deep vulnerability, being a mammal in this polar vortex, a silo of heat, amidst sub-zero paradise, lost.  Tested beyond any reasonable limit, we turned the key and scream-woke our car, rolled it downtown to the Open House for the Conservatory, pulverizing dirty, hapless ice rocks, and later trudged single-file past the Saint Paul Hotel and Rice Park back to our car, lights fixed at tangerine and neon blue.  Saw the Winter Carnival with no carnival, the empty skating rink, and fantastic ice sculptures with no visitors.  The dark "Emerald City" Landmark Center, its spires home to nothing tonight.  Restarted the car, waited, vanquished negative temperatures inside the steel capsule, and peered cautiously at a hopping and stopping, then again hopping young pedestrian, studying him for signs of mental illness.  Satisfied he would find shelter within minutes, we rolled back home, tucking the hood of the car into a snowbank, and reentered our heatdome, clapping, whisper-spitting-swearing, "Get off me!" at the hoary, irascible gray bastard, winter.

Battening down the hatches

Minnesota's classic January climate keeps huffing, puffing, and bulldozing from Embarrass, to Grand Forks, and Alexandria on down through the Twin Cities to Pepin, Wisconsin.  This is nothing like the cool breezes of recent Januaries that raise little goosebumps inside wool jumpers or nylon windbreakers.  These 20-45 mph squalls, multiplying the bracing negative *F air temperatures toward new records, roar over the tiptops of western fields, hurtling, swooping, sparkling sail sheets through shaking and orange-rusted, barbed-wire fences, broken-boarded barnyards, into black eyes of large animals who blink and shift their skinny feet.

Can't go a shivering day...

...without saying something.  So glum, unstructured, and wanton today, looking at papers and floor grit, stepping around it.  Sighing and snacking.  
     Today's cold (-16•f) reminded me of Milton's description of Hell in Paradise Lost as an endless lake of ice, its inhabitants wailing and gnashing their teeth, and permanently encased at approximately the thigh.  That's our breed of cold.  Cold as hell; not hot as hell. 
     The quality of this cold is quite impressive.  Either Minnesota has tilted its face up to the polar vortex or the vortex has slid down upon our face, but either way, we're earning encore visits with multiple days in the -teens to -20s below zero.  I figure our household will be paying back Xcel's averaging plan until 2015.   
     Shoes versus snow makes a styrofoam crunch.  I purse my lips to tolerate my own description.  Eyelashes go slushy.  The sun warms the threshold between doors, though, and painted doorwood gives off its oaky must.  
     Can tingling fingers be felt on this white window, glowing pinker with their bee-stung nerve endings?  Spots on the exterior foot arch inside socks and leather boots catch mini-cold waves, or chilly sweat swatches, millimeters from dry skin riverbeds of warm heel pads and pulsing big toenail beds.  Invisible feather boas brush past the feet released from boot-bondage, back inside the porch, kicked off, steam tufts rising from these same coursing "dogs."  Temperatures rising from -16•f to 70•f, regulators rock back and forth.  Now the sting seems cool water where none is.

Where Hotspur originates

     Sir Henry Percy KG (20 May 1364 – 21 July 1403), commonly known as Sir Harry Hotspur, or simply Hotspur, was a late medieval English nobleman. He was known as one of the most valiant knights of his day.  As a tribute to his speed in advance and readiness to attack' on the Scottish borders, the Scots bestowed on him the name 'Haatspore'.[2]  This is said to be the pennon captured by James Douglas, after Hotspur's death in combat, 1403.  


Hotspur figures prominently in Shakespeare's Henry IV, Part One, as an extremely skilled, able and hot-headed foe to the throne of Henry Bolingbroke, and it was maybe impulsive fire in his veins that dropped him in battle at Shrewsbury, going in short-handed to kill his king.  Below is a 19th century depiction of the shot that killed Hotspur.

Thursday, January 16, 2014

Stolen moments

     This morning at 6:30 while leaving the gas station and headed for my dark building on the opposite end of frozen Lake Como with blackness except for the glowing dashboard and snowy parkway, I felt impelled or impaled toward work by time.  Time that dots my arms like old cat-scratch scab lines, skinny, dried reminders to move it, go forward.  
     I can never predict this available slot, this luxury of expression.  The kids whisper inbetween tests, shuffle papers, adjust earbuds, sleep; they're dogged, similarly impelled, sarcastic, or flat asleep.  I'm like a 2x4, knocked back in my wheeled chair on scraped old tile floor, my mini fridge gurgling behind me with shrink-wrapped sandwich, chips and shrunken orange, the taste of stale SA coffee with Irish faux-cream scummed against the back roof of my throat.  A student just spoke, the elevator machinery that somehow lurks in my closet, blew hydraulic gas. 
     Time to sit up and go forward.  The bell approaches.  Ridiculous to picture oneself on a time ride or theater, or celluloid, locked in place, ticking inexorably closer to a bell.  But here it comes anyway!  They're off.
