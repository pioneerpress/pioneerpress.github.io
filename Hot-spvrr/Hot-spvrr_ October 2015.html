Jai'Sean 4

     You can just open the door to science class, and read this lab.
     Tearra wants the teacher to leave her alone.
     Mr. Jones carefully defends, "Excuse me, Tearra, but what kind of teacher would I be if I just left you alone?"  All small talk across the room stifles, eyes turn to Tearra, tag-team-teacher Mr. O'Shea slowly inches nearer to ease Tearra back down off her angry rocket.
     Jai'Sean can't restrain his court jester role to King Mr. Jones, and jumps out of his chair.
     "Now, you see?  Mister Jones."  He rounds the table, crossing inbetween Mr. Jones and Tearra, gesturing with both arms extended.  "I sincerely do tell you this, like eight times.  You need to not piss. Tearra. off.  Even I know that!  Learned my lesson!  Tearra don't play."
     Face lowered, Tearra eats her lips, but her eyes, full of lightning, crinkle, and a grin sneaks out around her long-braid extensions.
     Mr. Jones, flummoxed, blood-pressure medication strapping down his flushed face, leans on his knuckles and takes short breaths. 
     Jai'Sean spins back to his lab stool at the black counters, puts knuckles together over his chest and makes a heart symbol, and with phony love, aims it at Tearra.  "We good, girl?"
     "Sit back down, Jai'Sean," Mr. Jones says.  "You're being an idiot."
     "I just did you a solid, bro, and that's the thanks I get.  Go ahead, now it's all you."
     The science audience, plus several adults, all jerk-pan back and forth between Mr. Jones and Tearra, as if watching Wimbledon volleys.  Fireworks fizzle at the bell.

Teacher in a Buzz Saw

     We're in a wood chipper, a buzz saw, getting chopped every which way, at school.
     Nobody tells us teachers anything.  It's a news vacuum.  If there's a gun, or a new kid who brought a gun to his last school, we don't get to know.  We criss-cross our classrooms, communicating all day long, listening, leaning over knuckles like track runners on kids' desktops, imploring, encouraging, explaining, reteaching, and yet here we stand, in a god-damned news vacuum, saying, "What?  I didn't know that!"  
     Nobody tells us anything.
     There's the need to run a class and teach some content, against pitched obstruction from behaviors, some of it just horseplay, but too often ugly bullying, angry kid language, threats, physical attacks, and Snapchat to come videotape the next fight down the hall later today.  Perpetrators seem invincible.  We can say, "Huh-uh, no way."  "Oh yeah?  Yes way," seems to be the answer.
     We've got new faces this year, planted here by administration without comment how or why, and more new faces planted here this week and fuzzy description of their roles, and they fit in like a mindset transfusion to our building.  Thank you.  Are they Band-Aid People?  What are their authorities?  Who are they?  Like it or not, in an organization, people's roles need to be communicated.  If I'm planted here to help you, you need me to buy in, to consult me, regard me, respect me.  If you conversely  haven't consulted me, haven't explained what it means "to help out here and there," don't know exactly how or why you've transplanted bodies into our hallways, then don't expect them as anything more than Band-Aid people.  I don't know them; they don't know me.  I doubt I'm even glad they're here.
     There's the constant wish for peace by stopping, reaching out to hurting kids, just stopping class and questioning them, asking how they are, following up with more questions, just keeping the chat alive and going.  We do "touch and go," give more wait time, ask "How you doing?" to sleepers and the disconnected.
     Last, and worse than a "Lockdown-With-Intruder," there's survive-your-administrator mode, not just "don't get shot," but stay off administration radar.  You can be fired, tenured or not.  After you're repeatedly observed but not told why or what for, and an email where you showed frustration is forwarded to someone downtown, an administrator can say, "Set up a meeting with me to discuss your future," then threaten you behind closed doors.  And who wants to call a steward and draw more attention to yourself?
     This is the three-headed hound of Hell we face every day at school, due to NO SAFETY for anyone, and ill communication:  we need to 1) teach a class over wild behavior, 2) try to connect with hurting kids, and 3) avoid attention from administrators.
     Then someone likely says, "Geez, why you sound unhappy?  Maybe you're in the wrong business."
     No, I'm in a news vacuum, calling it as it looks.

Flyby Asteroid

     Sometimes the urge to make or break something, express ANYthing, comes up in you, up to your throat and cranial neural pathways, a Flying V bolt on your forehead, and it feels like caffeine, trembling ecstasy, hilarity, and rage, a dangerous speedball that, if left unsaid, just roars past like a flyby asteroid.  
     Sometimes it's weak, and slips past your mouth, never to be uttered. 
     Sometimes it overpowers you, and you have to break stuff, talk through, go to bed, do therapy.  
     Occasionally, it's just right, you line up your bat, and crush that speedball on the sweet spot out to the fences.
     No one knows the recipe for the richness of the gas to oxygen in the carburetor mix, but you know it when you jerk or tilt back to your canvas or guitar or writing pad.  Then you can feel it.  It's like striking a match so perfectly that it warms your skin while you crush the particles against the sandpaper, and yellow light gushes out the sides.

Jai'Sean 3

     At about 1:40 pm every day, 20 minutes till dismissal, it's home stretch for eleventh graders, the rigor of chemistry lifts, and occasional fun sneaks into class.
    "Mr., Mr. Whatever, Mr. O'Shea, how you do this?  I don't get this," Jai'Sean said.  He snapped his fingers three times -- "Over here!" -- as if in a French cafe, and Mr. O'Shea sneered, raised one eyebrow, and leaned to walk over, laser eyes on him.  
     "Sorry man, don't mean to disrespect."
     "Aw yeah ya did," his friend, Tearra said.  "He did mean to disrespect you, Mr. O'Shea."
     "I believe that, Tearra," he said.
     "Oh come on now.  I'm funnin'.  Just funnin'.
     "So what's up?" Mr. O'Shea asked.
     Jai'Sean looked down at the football video game going on his school-provided iPad.  He pressed pause, said, "Oh shit they scored!  Excuse me, those stupid motherfu__" and caught himself.
     "I'm-a walk away," Mr. O'Shea said, "ANND walk away with your iPad in a minute."
     "'K, sorr--sorr--sorry, but how the fuck you do this?  'Scuse me 'gain, but how, what is, 'Conservation of Mass explained by acid and a base and a fuckin' pardon me again balloon?  'Cause I know about you can't create or destroy mass, they gotta equal out, but what?"
     Tearra watched with her chin forward across the matte black lab tabletop, resting on her stacked, closed fists, nice and comfortably.  Her eyelids dropped and she grinned.
     "You do not know what the fuck you talkin' 'bout," Jai'Sean," she said.
     "Excuse me, can we find any other words to speak politely around here?"  They both agreed to try, and snickered up their sinuses.
     "'You never call me on my cell phone,'" Jai'Sean sang, then thought of his past weekend.  "So!  So!  I texted her, 'Do ya wanna do twos?'  I mean like, she could drive over, bring her guy, and get with me and my girl, and do a little extra, you know, turn up?"
     Mr. O'Shea walked that crazy bleeding edge of, "Don't let it go -- let it go -- see if that's all."  He looked at Tearra, she made a "Tch" with her tooth.
     "Boy, you prefer she don't bring her boyfriend," Tearra said.  They snarked it up.
     "How you know me so well?" he said.  "And now, I finna fight 'em."
     "You don't gotta fight 'em.  You don't even know no Apps."
     "Apps?" Mr. O'Shea said.
     "Like Opps [opponents], but it's Minneapolis, so it's Apps, and I am gonna fight 'em."
     Mr. O'Shea called out the phone exchange challenge, with DJ enunciation:  "6-1-2... 6-1-2."
     "We ALL just 6-5-1 over Saint Paulee, Mr. O'Shea."
     The bell rang.  Kids 1, chemistry 0.
     "Please put up your chairs!" Mr. O'Shea called.  Jai'Sean and Tearra both stayed and put up chairs, seemed fine to help out, and Mr. O'Shea wondered at the serenity at their foreheads, feeling fine if they were fine.

some political jive I'm trying out

Now that even the right-wing Pioneer Press has thrown in for the DFL-endorsed four school board candidates, I say great.  I hope Mary Vanderwert, Zuki Ellis, Steve Marchese, and Jon Schumacher are elected.  I've got their backs on Nov. 4.  Here's what I want them to understand about being an employee in SPPS.

I'm dreaming of a sea change in Saint Paul schools towards a New Equity, one that wipes out autocracy, erases bias, and bridges gaps.   Racial equity has caught hold and will stick around after Pacific Education Group has left, and we should keep embracing it, even as we embrace the other lives that also Matter:  yes, Native, Asian, Latino, and African Lives.  

But what I'm talking about is the autocracy, the bias, and gap between schools and families, students and teachers, and "leadership" and staff.  In my dream of The New Equity, there's no you versus me, no us vs. them, students vs. teachers, parents vs. schools.  We're all pulling for kids' success, even if kids sometimes drag on the rope, and parents sometimes can't show up.  I want a chair for them at the table, because they're on our team.

One of the symptoms of a happy, finely tuned organization is its responsiveness to employees, having their backs, not just allowing their voices to be heard, but insisting they be heard by relinquishing and sharing leadership so that decisions fall to small groups or teams.  No, this does not already exist in Saint Paul schools.  

That's why I dream that decision making about budgets, staffing, curriculum contracts with companies we keep or let expire, professional development, start times, where kids eat breakfast, etc., gets yanked back and shared with the schools, to committees made of cross sections of adults, parents, and kids.

In this dream, there's no cabinet downtown.  There are servant leaders down there.  No need for transparency, because transparency assumes opacity.

We're working in a complicated, chaotic, but seriously top-down climate, one that doesn't understand, can't communicate sincerely nor effectively, wouldn't even broach shared decision making.

If all four are elected on November 4, I say great.  Whoever makes up our new board needs to know that while we have their backs, they better have ours; they cannot warp into rubber stamping bobbleheads.

Commandant

     Sometimes you just don't know where your mouthfuls of words come from, their colors, their blackness, how little or much to say, what amorphous seedling / feelings grew them, and why you presume there's some audience to receive them.
     You're unsure whether your narrative should stand in the shoes of student seeiing a teacher, to different teacher, to parent, to friend of teacher, to Mr. O'Shea, or Mr. Fred.  How much trouble could you face?  Is your story redundant, such that you haven't already told it?  
     You've told a lot of ugliness already, and ugliness has its home in the lexicon of expression.  You'll  lose people after a bit.
     But this ugliness today cannot be snuffed or subjugated.
     I'm just so nauseous with it that I can barely keep the bile down.
     Our department held its once a month meeting at 2:15, and our downtown administrator appeared a half hour late, then kept us until past 3:30, berating, belittling, cudgeling.
     She came and stated her purpose, to give updates to what she's seen in a few walk-throughs in study hall classes for special ed kids.  It quickly kicked into gear with a stand-up Post-It note, find a partner, write your favorite thing about AVID strategies, even if you don't teach it, and then to ask what you'd like to learn more about, then it devolved into a pacing, cross examination, where she picked on people to answer, took questions, claimed she knew the question was coming, paced some more, accused people of not smiling, said she had some deep concerns, "Y'all are getting me concerned!"  Can we get some smiles here?"  She paced, flashed her crocodile smile, absolutely insisted that everyone -- NO, EVERYONE HAD BEEN TRAINED!
     Is it any wonder that I take weeklong breaks from copying down this garbage?  I can't stand it because it's so like a protracted cross examination, even a water boarding at Abu Ghraib?  I get nauseous when I write about it. 
     Someone needs to write it down, however.

Dad's squint

     ... in the sun automatically turned him a bit ferocious.  Just a notch.  When Dad squinted.  His eyes became asterisks, his eyebrows tied by inside-out parentheses )(.  His jawline squared.  Did others fear that look? I used to wonder.  Did they read him wrong?  I thought he better soften himself while in the sun, lest others saw some anger where none was ventured.  Not a sustained thought.  I'm just seeing my face in the sun, and I also grimace when I'm lit.  Kind of my endlessly over-sensitive innards.
     Also, Dad's knuckles on the steering wheel.  He slung his index and middle fingers over the bottom of the wheel arc, and hung them there.  I remember the glimmering tan plastic wheel, and his skin color:  pink-orange-brown, shiny at the swollen knuckles, a few hairs in wiry mayhem, his whole navigational system relaxed.  He often wore a light-colored check, "Farewell Iron, Wrinkle-Free, Perma-Press" shirt.
     Dad had bigger shoulders than mine, but scooped inward, and a methodical gait with out-turned toes that asked the world for a little help with forward progress.  "Give me a little descent," he seemed to ask the earth.
     Oh and I didn't mention my dad had tons more hair, white stuff, which he scooped back with his fingers.

Black crows and tire gators

     Wanting to lessen the pain in the world again.  Increase the happiness of a few again.  Emerging from a weekend with aerial crows and highway crows (those so-called truck "tire gators" that shred off old semis and scatter jaggedly to the shoulders), I'm suddenly seeing things in them that scare me.  Over the weekend, when I saw them, they were clever, social birds, and black freeway sculptures; now I see some kind of frightening things out of the corners of my warped, cheap eyeglasses.
     That's the closest I come to Halloween writing.  Realizing that part of this inspiration came from having seen some creepy things online just now, just by pointing and clicking, that's why I've written.
     The real underlying fear oozing up inside me is tomorrow's return to school, the return to the potential for drive-by observations, those instances where authorities walk in and catch me doing little, or kids doing little, or nothing educational visible.
     Anyway, where are those flying crows up north now, circling, laughing, tilting, heads wagging, and beaks scolding over a scrap of bread on the roadside?  Is the water in the Devil's Kettle pounding just as hard into its abyss now at midnight as when I saw it on Friday?
     Thinking of all the people I miss, all the times I miss, all the irrecoverable sensations to pass scorching through my senses. 
     The myriad beats of 1,000 songs played on 1,000 chariots circling and dipping down from constellations, songs I once sang, but can't raise my throat now, it's so raw and tired.
     Anyway, it's late, the blackness invites and envelopes and snuffs every candle.

Ke'Trail dusted up

     Sometimes, Mr. O'Shea, whomp.  You gotta just tell all you saw, get it out, get it off the short-term memory to the white space of this box.

     Mr. O'Shea jumped from his aluminum swivel chair, and ran out of his room to break up the fight.

The spine of the story

     Say.  Up in here you'll find a batch of fresh sketches about school kids.  I'm Tom O'Shea, a frowsy, middle-aged, North End High School, Saint Paul, special education teacher.  Hundreds of kids in 15 years have counted me as their teacher, but I only just decided to tell their little train wrecks, to shove them up here like sound polaroids, best I could, in Realface, Realvoice -- kind of like Dolby.  Just listen.  It's not easy transcribing exact speech, but it's a worthwhile combo of science-art.  
     City kids now, they're like dirt underfoot when you first meet them, society's garbage; then suddenly they twist clumsy pliÃ©s into grace, like vases on the shelf in glass blowing 101 class.  Some smash, but almost every kid cranks into a pretty nice, fluted bottle.
     Every school is made of cinderblocks, glossy painted.  Echo is dampened by white speckled, acoustical ceiling tiles, and low-pile gray carpeting, or nothing:  just square tiles, squiggly with gray and pink boomerangs.  In the commons, kids pass like rainbow ramen in a giant spaghetti pot, and I'm shouldering past, too, always gripped, locked by their transactions carrying them by seconds to minutes to hours to the last June day of school, pulling their stupid stunts (same as my stupid crap at that age), performing some tragic or hilarious antic.  
     Seems like I popped out of graduate school last week, and don't know jack about kids, because I lose hours of sleep and years of life.  Sunday nights still whack me down to three or four hours' sleep.  Honestly, I've been doing this since the last century, so you'd think I knew something.  I don't retain much of it, can't recall, on-demand, too many anecdotes, but rather, they come to me at school.  Just right there in the gene pool.  I sort of light up and shimmer when I'm "in the building," coaxing kids, "C'mon!  Learn!" We jostle and box for 6 1/2 hours, and my luster dims like blown glass when the burner's off.  So.  Who remembers anything?  I don't, especially at this age, so that's why I scramble with thumb pecks on my digital device to record it all on the spot, so that's what you're getting.  Fresh sketches.
     Spend your time around kids, and if you don't catch bird flu, you're eternally fixed, stupefied.  I just wish I'd been a social worker like my dad, because I ain't cut out for this classroom mess, a massive game of fumble-itis with 1,500 kids.  I say fumble-itis, like "smear the football carrier" where you just razzle-dazzle-drop-it when tackled, because really, I haven't worked in a building that wasn't insane, in near-riot mode.  Check that: my first Saint Paul job after I taught in Minneapolis, as Poetry Guy and half-time kindergarten teacher, at happy little, reconstituted, Pine Cone Elementary art school, ran like a hummingbird for two years while I was there.  The principal led while prostrate atop the office counter with her eyes closed, assenting to permission requests for artists in residence, grants for Orff instruments, movable theater scrims, ballet bars, potting wheels, and glitter glue.  Grants were transfusions.  But actual books?  Pssh, we raided Half Price Book dumpsters.
     Chopped funds cost me that job.  Chopped funds cost me the next job, and the one after that.  I worked with kindergartners until a federal grant earned me a special education license, and I scooted over here to North End.  When you see five to 18-year-olds, you really watch them come in like tired little sweeties, and then exit like hulking, bearded Frankensteins and young women hobbling on horrid heels and unfortunate leggings.
     Here's my thing about these sketches:  should I have editorialized about the school environment?  Blunt it back so you're not offended and chuck the book?  Do I beautify it by pumping myself up to cathedral levels of chippy-chap happiness?  Sparkling with inside-out, self aggrandizing bubbliness?  Never mind the true fact we have teenage gunslingers onsite every day, police tell us.  Yeah, I know my gangbangers.  They tell me.  I had two chrome packers on my caseload last year, charged with carrying at their last building.  This year, two other kids have escaped getting caught, carrying pieces inside, before handing off to someone else.  Excuse the colloquials, but my vernacular has been molded and modified.  What's amazing is that it's just part of teachers' day, part of kids' day.  We're on self-selected orange alert, all the time.
     Thing of it is, paranoia about administration ranks higher than fear of guns to me. You'd think nothing rivals that incessant orange alert, right?  Nothing causing PTSD on that par?  Well yes, in fact, try being surprised 25 times a year by half a dozen administrators checking off Google Sheets that justify their jobs, observing you without feedback, saying only it's for Data, recording on their iPads what kids tell them in whispered confidence right in front of you.  When you do know they're coming tomorrow morning, you want to die.
     No, it sucks to get shot, but being observed can really wreck your day.
     Anyway, enough prologue.  
     Most of these bits were my perspective, but some were interior monologues by kids, imagined by me, and some by friends who sat in and saw for themselves.  Here's to the almost-400 I've taught in 15 years, and the thousands I've known.  I really like these kids, every last one of them.  They're crazy.  I wish to God I could save them all.

The number 5

     The number 5 is red alive
     The number 4 is green
     The number 3 is yellowing
     The number 2 is blue
     The number one is white 
     Because alone can't stand a color

     I don't know why.  I don't know.  In my toddlerhood, numbers had colors. Letters were too many to assign... maybe they had smells.  As if ginger or lemon peel scraped off the letter E.  The noisy pepper that sprinkles off a big capital K.  Letters are too loud and soft.  I loved the calm curve of 5 and the mix of burnt umber in yellow 3 and rusty 7.
     But I guess no one knows how to say a shape scratched with graphite onto paper.  How did the Chinese learn to speak a character?  It doesn't make a sound.  Scratches, only.  Just two processes side by side, sound and vision.  No one knows how.  No one knows.  It's that speech processing center of the universal human mind, multiplied by all souls, which churns and pops syllables while drawing with pencils.  A disjointed dual process.  No one knows how that goes, and yet....
     Someone lights a fire on a beach, it crackles, orange flickering legs cross and bottoms wiggle close together, all eyes turn to the story teller, and out of her mouth comes brain quelling, lovely "once upon a time(s)."
     There.  I said something.
     It's really hard for me to speak sometimes.
