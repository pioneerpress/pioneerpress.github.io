Waking detritus

     Vowed when I started this blog I'd sequester it far from the real world, far from daily strife, and save it for my sweetest musings and purplest troubles, but I have to release bits of reality.  
     I slept on and off and on since yesterday, waking to the plucking harp of this phone, interrupting a dream, a 1:1 staredown with a giant Latino gay muscleman, not musician; a muscleman, working on the machinery of a hotel elevator, and I didn't know if he was gay until he opened his mouth and said something oddly soft and self deprecating about the kind of day he was having.  
     And I woke up because it's not polite to stare and try to figure someone out without engaging in conversation, so I woke, and I thought immediately about school suicide attempts and guns, and 2-minute fights on closed-circuit camera that no adult stops, and the grim demand from my people downtown, not explicitly to stop any of that from happening, all of which are near and fearsome to my heart today, but to complete the paperwork that covers the asses of people downtown who can cross you off their list as having spoken to you about paperwork so that you no longer exist to them.  But do not question nor ask clarification from these people because they will turn around and place you on an improvement plan.  So, understand.  "You've been spoken to.  Now do your job," is the ethos.  
     So I hummed soundlessly -- maybe with a little breath -- to myself in the dark of my bedroom, buttoning my shirt, 

"You have to get right out of here
You have to get away
I had to pay the doorman
Just to let me use the door
I had to use a muscleman
To pick me off the floor."
     By now, many minutes after beginning this entry, I've forgotten all the little bits that came to me.  They're scampering off to corners; they get pushed aside by new musings and clutter. No matter, it was just words.  Words are all I have. I've got more of them.  They'll come in a new daisy chain or gum wrapper chain.

When I come around

     I know it's a song lyric, but I feel myself on a great iron-toothed gear, maybe an old diesel-powered, wood splitting gear.  I don't even know what that looks like; I only know I feel I'm riding a great gear, a Charlie Chaplin, "Modern Times" gear, one in which it's not safe to get caught.  Or Harold Lloyd in "Safety Last," hanging from the clock.  Still too funny.  
     So, a horrible fairground ride, with exponentially spiking and sinking G forces, a Crack the Whip, Mad Mouse, shoved against interior dented walls of chipped paint steel, studying the scraped colors of powder blue, powder pink, and pinstripe green.  All chippy, dinged, and dented.
     I'm coming around.  Sometime in the near future, I'll pass the crux, the hub, the core of the concern, and snap my flywheel back out around the galaxy of terminal blood flow before losing consciousness.  
     I would continue by posting the reality stake holdings of this tonal painting, but it's so unbelievably ugly, I can't bear to look at the canvas I paint.  Waiting for the fly wheel to snap me back around.

     I can't hold many images like the hayrake below without crushing sadness.  I don't know why.  I want to flow through these tines, to feel those blades of timothy and alfalfa again in my fingers.  I need the satisfaction of making that land fall into 60# bales that totter up the auger into the blackness of the barn.  But I can't apprehend the images long before I swoon in despair of lonesome memories I cannot properly convey to anyone outside.  So....  So it goes.  And so they go, fading in my memory.  Gears.  Flywheels.  Rust.  Sweaty knuckles, the burning of seeds and dust overwhelming nose cilia or hair.  Sneezing.  The shiny green flies on the fresh piles of manure.  Gotta stop this....

Saturday, February 8, 2014

Performance

There's a performance quality to scrivening that really hurts to toss onstage some days, because that's a shiny spot, and my days feature loose game chips of ugliness on my chessboard:  a kaleidoscopic nature puzzle with puzzle pieces from the wrong nature puzzle.  Do you feel me?  It's not chess to me some days; it's more like checkers; no, tic-tac-toe; no, 52-card pick-up.  I'm just telling you this.  I'm just talking.  All right?  Maybe my testifying impulse with words will keep balance.  I just want you to know that I am not going to paint pretty pictures.  Dostoevsky began his "Notes From The Underground" with "I am a sick man.  I am a spiteful man.  I am an unpleasant man.  I believe my liver is diseased...." to be continued.  I don't know that I can revisit this piece.  But my original impulse was to say I'm shy, because I'm often far from the limelight of any blessings, so my work will often reside outside behind the back alley stage door.  Where it's cold and wet.  That's where I frequently sit in the cold.  I'm not going to ignore the alley or its menace.  I actually like to work it through my body.  However, I woke today with the fluffy vibrating cuticles of enough sleep.  The paralysis in pink fingertips.  The unknitted eyebrows.  The warmth in calm skin.

Whether 'tis nobler

     Yes, I know that's a Hamlet splice, but what's nobler when nobility seems faded and unattainable?  Ignoble, really.  Just jotting thoughts, and watching this f*ing app lose them!  Riffing on some of my favorite lyrics in literary history, like the one below.  
     Coleridge's poem below, I experienced at age 19 in one my English romantic period classes.  What pouty 19 year old wouldn't love pieces like below?  
     I'm riffing on a new image I hold, of a tree.  This tree would be a meek, patient, hundreds year old olive, fig, or almond tree, at least one that couldn't survive this northern climate.  Yet it could survive for centuries on a hot, baked ridge of boulder, breathing air, produce a few fruits and live, dusty and creaking in heat and wind, rattling its rough leaves against one another.  
     One afternoon finally, clouds mount over a hillside, stack up, roll as black bowling balls downhill, and begin to rain on this little tree, dabbing dust off it.  Rain picks up, and drenches the tree, quenches the roots and surfeits the soil.  The tree rocks from base to tip, now supple in its footings.  Just now a single crack of white heat meets the earth at the tree, and splits the trunk to the dirt.  Its trunk flies apart in a powder explosion, and each half touches earth never felt in its life, splayed wide open to the sky.  Its gardener, hours later, discovers it, picks up the halves which sigh upward at nothing, yet still attached to its gasping ganglia root ball.  He sprays the tan wounded heartwood with black tar sealant, lifts up each broken half, himself wincing at contrast between dried bark and moist interior.  With twine and wire, he wraps the tree back together.  The tree could be dead or alive.  Whatever it had once known about itself, whatever small bubble of awareness it'd had is now gone; it's fallen into a tree coma, a post-operative delirium, and no longer can hold itself up.  It doesn't know what it is, a vegetable or mineral.  The gardener stakes it off with twine, and the tree stands again, trembling and senseless.  He hopes the tree will fuse, and it does.  It takes 10 years.  The twine and wire and stakes hold, the tree holds, taking its own measure again, neither apologizing nor boasting, but standing. It'll never again yield fruit; it'll never stand without crutches and braces, but it doesn't die, not yet.




This Lime-tree Bower my Prison

BY SAMUEL TAYLOR COLERIDGE

[Addressed to Charles Lamb, of the India House, London]

Well, they are gone, and here must I remain,
This lime-tree bower my prison! I have lost
Beauties and feelings, such as would have been
Most sweet to my remembrance even when age
Had dimm'd mine eyes to blindness! They, meanwhile,
Friends, whom I never more may meet again,
On springy heath, along the hill-top edge,
Wander in gladness, and wind down, perchance,
To that still roaring dell, of which I told;
The roaring dell, o'erwooded, narrow, deep,
And only speckled by the mid-day sun;
Where its slim trunk the ash from rock to rock
Flings arching like a bridge;—that branchless ash,
Unsunn'd and damp, whose few poor yellow leaves
Ne'er tremble in the gale, yet tremble still,
Fann'd by the water-fall! and there my friends
Behold the dark green file of long lank weeds,
That all at once (a most fantastic sight!)
Still nod and drip beneath the dripping edge
Of the blue clay-stone.

                        Now, my friends emerge
Beneath the wide wide Heaven—and view again
The many-steepled tract magnificent
Of hilly fields and meadows, and the sea,
With some fair bark, perhaps, whose sails light up
The slip of smooth clear blue betwixt two Isles
Of purple shadow! Yes! they wander on
In gladness all; but thou, methinks, most glad,
My gentle-hearted Charles! for thou hast pined
And hunger'd after Nature, many a year,
In the great City pent, winning thy way
With sad yet patient soul, through evil and pain
And strange calamity! Ah! slowly sink
Behind the western ridge, thou glorious Sun!
Shine in the slant beams of the sinking orb,
Ye purple heath-flowers! richlier burn, ye clouds!
Live in the yellow light, ye distant groves!
And kindle, thou blue Ocean! So my friend
Struck with deep joy may stand, as I have stood,
Silent with swimming sense; yea, gazing round
On the wide landscape, gaze till all doth seem
Less gross than bodily; and of such hues
As veil the Almighty Spirit, when yet he makes
Spirits perceive his presence.

                        A delight
Comes sudden on my heart, and I am glad
As I myself were there! Nor in this bower,
This little lime-tree bower, have I not mark'd
Much that has sooth'd me. Pale beneath the blaze
Hung the transparent foliage; and I watch'd
Some broad and sunny leaf, and lov'd to see
The shadow of the leaf and stem above
Dappling its sunshine! And that walnut-tree
Was richly ting'd, and a deep radiance lay
Full on the ancient ivy, which usurps
Those fronting elms, and now, with blackest mass
Makes their dark branches gleam a lighter hue
Through the late twilight: and though now the bat
Wheels silent by, and not a swallow twitters,
Yet still the solitary humble-bee
Sings in the bean-flower! Henceforth I shall know
That Nature ne'er deserts the wise and pure;
No plot so narrow, be but Nature there,
No waste so vacant, but may well employ
Each faculty of sense, and keep the heart
Awake to Love and Beauty! and sometimes
'Tis well to be bereft of promis'd good,
That we may lift the soul, and contemplate
With lively joy the joys we cannot share.
My gentle-hearted Charles! when the last rook
Beat its straight path along the dusky air
Homewards, I blest it! deeming its black wing
(Now a dim speck, now vanishing in light)
Had cross'd the mighty Orb's dilated glory,
While thou stood'st gazing; or, when all was still,
Flew creeking o'er thy head, and had a charm
For thee, my gentle-hearted Charles, to whom
No sound is dissonant which tells of Life.

shinny up falling rock

Shinny up falling rock
grab hold but can't stop
Scunned elbows and torn knuckles
broken toes and flattened nose
sharp pang shinnying
shredded sinews winnowing 
I'm over under sideways down
nothing stop a rock 

And all I want to do is stop

it's not a good day
for what-are-coloring inside lines
if you can call it that
splashing a mess around
blink but really nothing's done, 
push the forward crayon 
no furrow left behind
all in the mind
move until paralyzed

just want to quit forcing

and the point's still sharp
ready to plow 
because it hasn't been used
not really
if it were, it would show
behind the teeth
under the eyelids
in the skin
there's really no blood
no sinew 
no rock stoppage
