Sunday, February 7, 2016

Whitecaps on the ocean

     --considered while driving up Ford Parkway in Saint Paul, MN.

     No matter how I brandish my quills, my quiver of fiery, bristling arrows, they all fall dead on fallow pages, missing the mark of a person's black-eyed smile, her crow's feet, the diamond or pecan shape of her face, a boy's delight in a service dog in a grocery store slapped aside in terror and tears by its bark, just the cadence of speech.  There's no way printed words, the crow's feet of paper, could capture trills or diminuendo, the subtlety of monotonous delivery punctuated by a tiny upward blip.  Maybe, like photo film, something appears over time.  I don't know how to do it.  I've got no camera for it, yet my eyes are always fixated on those distant whitecaps on the ocean under the sun, that mechanized hare in the greyhound race.  
Dropping lines, feeing kinship. Always unequal to take full soundings.

Le'Vante

This is old.

"You ain't know me" was all Mr. O'Shea ever got out of Le'Vante.  True that.  Mr. O'Shea didn't see him enough to get to know him.  They sent him to "homebound behavioral education" after Le'Vante brought a knife to school.  He brought a gun to his last school.  Super handsome smile for his 14-year-old school pic, taken the previous September.  No short dreads yet, no cap, no hood.
"You ain't gon' know me either."
Hearing that, in their family meeting, the team leaned in on their elbows and sighed heavily.  They tried to explain their responsibility to Mom.  Responsibility to protect the other 1,400 kids in the high school.  
    She bombed Le'Vante with 10 minutes of acid vitriol, the likes of which, well -- "WHAT THE FUCK YOU DOIN' TO ME?  I BROUGHT YOU UP GAVE YOU EVERYTHING I HAD AND SOME!  YOU GON' FUCKIN' TREAT ME LIKE THIS, MUTHAFUCKA?"  Sobbing, long inhale, then, "THEY JUST DOIN' THEY JOB!  GIVING YOUR LITTLE MUTHAFUCKIN' ASS ANOTHER CHANCE AND NOW THIS?  BREAKING MY HEART!"  Mom had giant tears dropping off her cheeks, coursing from her tomato eyes.
   You've seen O.J.'s slow-speed chase, right?  Le'Vante led adults on numerous slow-speed chases through the high school, telling anyone who tailed him, "Get offa my dick 'fore I fuck you up."  Which guaranteed close tailing, write-up, and dismissal.  After he brought the knife to school....  After he brought the gun to his last school.
"Tried so hard, man," case manager Mr. O'Shea told his mom, in front of the pale-faced table of six district rep's.  "We've all been trying... and you've tried so hard."
  Her shoulders shivered in more sobs.
Le'Vante hunched over sideways down at the end, hood up, right elbow on table, occasionally adjusting the laces on his stunning red sneakers.  "See? they sayin'"... he murmured.  He held forth with his audience of sand on the floor, gently skidding grit with his soles like windshield wipers.  
"Excuse me?" Mr. O'Shea asked.  "Le'Vante would you--?"
Mr. Dean cut off discussion and recited that Le'Vante and his mom would go back home and await a phone call from the school about his future education.
Security cameras had shown that Le'Vante offered the knife directly to Mr. Dean.  The clear black-and-white video had shown the cause of the previous suspension, Le'Vante in a five-minute fight no adult had stopped, had previously shown Le'Vante pacing up and down the empty hallway, pants sagging, knees outward to prevent their total drop to the floor, his hands over his hooded head, zigzagging.  "What's he doing there?" the team had puzzled.  Fast-forward tape.  Rewind.  Le'Vante zigzagged faster.  "He's not waiting for anyone to fight.  He doesn't know anyone.  He's avoiding, maybe.  Oh here come the other boys.  Watch."  One boy had been nominated to take down Le'Vante.  When he'd been questioned why later, that boy admitted he had no reason to fight Le'Vante.  Just that he's a boxer, he likes to box, and his homes told him to.  So they found Le'Vante and tried to take him down.  No damage was done.
Security cameras in eighth grade had shown Le'Vante offered his backpack to an administrator and pointed into it.  The admin. pulled out the gun.  Bye, Le'Vante.
Special education teachers try to get at what motivates kids to act the way they do, and some guessed Le'Vante had beginnings of delusions, wanted protection from perceived threats in schools via suspension, and he knew no safer route than to threaten others.
Which didn't explain why his 110-pound frame with sweet smile would pinpoint more vulnerable kids in the bathroom and jack money off them.  Even while escorted down the hall by the huge paraprofessional with massive street cred, Mr. Davis.
"I'ma get my money, muthafucka!"
"Le'Vante! Mr. Davis had said, "I'm standin' right here, man!"

     The team stared silently.
     "You ain't gon' know me" was Le'Vante's whispered closing.  Mr. O'Shea, mouth pursed and tears in his eyes, only prayed he wouldn't.

Tuesday, February 2, 2016

In a silent way.

     Chill, as Miles Davis wrote it. Miles of blue silence inside these white walls of reticence, trying to roll around the inside of a quiet barn.  It's good to cam down.  No more engagement on social media whatsoever for awhile.  Going on three days now, and it feels clear. Soft, like summer water at the lake of my childhood, little flecks of algae suspended and lolling like vitamin e geltabs.  I miss that soft warm water and the fat lazy sunfish. Want to float stomach down on a neoprene yellow air mattress right now, feeling each baby-loft on the waves. Anyway, it's time to sleep.
